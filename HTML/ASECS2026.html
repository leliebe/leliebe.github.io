New Work Using the London Stage Database

ASECS 2026

Lauren Liebe, Penn State Behrend

This is a story of several different digital humanities projects and my efforts to bring them all into conversation with one another. If, at moments, it seems that I am straying away from the topic of the London Stage Database, please bear with me.

I first became aware of the London Stage Database project in the midst of my PhD preliminary examination defense. It was near the end, when we had shifted to talking about what the dissertation might look like. I knew I wanted a digital component, and I had two ideas: TEI-encoding the primary Restoration-era playtexts that I was working with, or creating a more readily searchable and filterable edition of The London Stage.

Luckily, a member of my dissertation committee, Laura Estill, knew about Mattie’s project—which, with its media archaeology components, is far cooler than what I was proposing. Laura urged me to contact Mattie, and I am immensely glad that I did. It was, I think, a fortuitous meeting for both of us—The London Stage’s Restoration-era data is notoriously messy and inconsistently structured compared to the rest of the volumes. I joined the advisory board as a subject-matter expert, and my work with the LSDB was immensely helpful to my own growth as a scholar of Restoration drama and digital humanities.

Of course, with a digitization project on The London Stage already well underway, this freed me to work on the other proposed project: a TEI-encoded set of playtexts I was using in my dissertation. While many of these texts were already part of the XML-encoded EEBO-TCP project, I was much more interested in building a more nuanced apparatus, one which specifically focused on the people involved in these productions—the actors, dramatists, theater managers, and musicians—as well as the historical records surrounding these plays. For a period in which theatre history is largely fragmentary, being able to trace the sources of our information is crucial, which is where The London Stage comes into play, along with later works like Milhous and Hume’s Register of Theatrical Documents.

My dissertation project, then became Digital Restoration Drama, and by the time I graduated, it included eight plays all linked via a SQL database of people, places, characters, and texts that was intended to eventually link together a complete corpus of plays published or performed between 1660-1685, or the reign of Charles II.

Digital Restoration Drama, in its initial incarnation, was definitely a success, but it was also very much a learning experience. Some of what it achieved—particularly the underlying SQL database—was a bit overly engineered for what it was attempting to do. It was also, like so many digital humanities projects, rather siloed off in its own little world. I had always imagined connecting to the LSDB once my project was mature enough, and when I learned about the Linked Early Modern Drama Online project—or LEMDO—during my postdoc, I thought that it, too, might be a good way to make my project more visible and interoperable with others.

This leads us to present day. Digital Restoration Drama is currently offline as I rework the encoding and expand the critical apparatus to bring it into line with LEMDO’s standards. LEMDO uses XML to do much of what I accomplished with SQL, meaning that I am substantially revising the data structure underlying my initial work. For the purposes of bringing my work into connection with the LSDB, though, this also brings its own complications. LEMDO uses two different structures for identifying people: a personography for modern academics and other people contributing to the project, and a prosopography for all of the historical figures mentioned in the plays or their paratexts.

While the LEMDO structures include some level of certainty across LEMDO projects via internal xml:id tags, this data could be improved through the addition of linked open data markers...as could the vast number of individuals who appear throughout the LSDB. Effectively, linked open data—pointers to VIAF, Wikidata, and the like—could make these projects interoperable, both with one another and with other projects also applying linked open data, like Theatronomics or those included in the LINCS project out of the University of Guelph.

Right now, these projects all include only partial areas of overlap. However, the more projects that adopt linked open data, the broader our picture becomes of just how interconnected these projects and the individuals whose lives they cover truly are.

This was my role in the LSDB’s currently suspended NEH grant project: to disambiguate the thousands of named individuals—performers, theater managers, musicians, dramatists, dancers—and either connect them to pre-existing URIs or create new ones. This work is especially important for the theatre world of the eighteenth century, where theatre was not infrequently a family business, leading to unclear records (but which Cibber is it?). Linked open data allows us to connect each individual to a canonical entry, allowing for consistency and clarity—or at least an acknowledgement of uncertainty—both internally and across multiple projects, thus leading to (hopefully) easier research on these individuals and their networks. It also helps specifically with clarifying the London Stage’s ladder system, encoded into the original books in a way that can occasionally obfuscate who is actually involved in a particular performance.

So, what does all of this mean for the potential future of the LSDB and Digital Restoration Drama? Right now, when you visit the LSDB, you can click on a performer’s name and run a search on that performer. This search is looking for a matching string, not a disambiguated entity. For example, if we click on “Smith,” we end up with 5720 results with performance dates starting in 1662 and ending in 1800. Disambiguated data would allow us to break this “Smith” into the multiple performers surely being represented here. And by using linked open data as part of that disambiguation process, we can link each of those “Smiths” to their appearances elsewhere, whether that be in Digital Restoration Drama and LEMDO as a whole, Theatronomics, LINCS, or other projects that have yet to be imagined.

Thank you.
